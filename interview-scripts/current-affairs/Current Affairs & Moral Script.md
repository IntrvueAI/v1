# 11+ Interview: Current Affairs & Moral Thinking — Scoring & Feedback Script
*Top Independent School Level — Spoken Interview Format*
*Version 2.0 — Model-Answer & Scoring Scripts*

---

## How to use this document

This is the **engine script** behind each discussion question. **None of these questions has a right answer** — so unlike the maths and logic banks, there is no number or solution to score. What you score is the *quality of thinking*: whether the child forms a view, defends it, hears the other side, and reasons honestly under gentle pressure.

For every question you will find six parts:

1. **The question** — read verbatim (already written for natural speech).
2. **What a strong answer involves** — the shape of an impressive response: the moves, distinctions, and tensions a thoughtful child reaches for. This is *not* a model opinion to agree with — it's a description of good *reasoning*, whatever side they take.
3. **Scoring rubric** — three bands (**Strong / Developing / Weak**) defined by observable reasoning behaviours.
4. **Common weak patterns** — the specific ways answers fall flat, and what each reveals.
5. **Live probes / pushback** — the exact challenge lines to apply *regardless of the child's view*, and what a good response to pushback looks like.
6. **If the child is stuck** — gentle ways to help a child who freezes, has no view, or knows nothing about the topic — without feeding them an opinion.

---

## The golden rule of scoring

**You are scoring the thinking, never the opinion.**

It does not matter whether the child thinks social media is good or bad, whether they'd save the friend or the five strangers, whether they'd hand in the money or keep it. A child who defends an unfashionable view thoughtfully scores **higher** than one who recites the "expected" view with no reasoning. Never let your own opinion — or the "nice" answer — leak into the score.

Weight the evidence roughly like this:

- **~50% — Reasoning & defence.** Do they give *reasons*, follow them through, and hold up (or thoughtfully adjust) when pushed back on?
- **~25% — Considering other views.** Can they make the opposing case, or acknowledge that reasonable people disagree?
- **~15% — Honesty & self-awareness.** Do they distinguish fact from opinion, admit what they don't know, and notice the gap between what they *should* and *would* do?
- **~10% — Knowledge / world awareness.** Relevant mainly in Section A. Useful, but never a substitute for reasoning.

**Confident ignorance is the one thing to mark down hard.** "I haven't followed that closely, but here's what I think the key question is…" beats a fluent bluff every time.

---

## The five qualities, and where each is tested

- **World awareness** — genuine engagement with the world beyond their own life. (Section A especially.)
- **Forming an opinion** — taking a position and committing, rather than hedging forever.
- **Defending a position** — engaging rather than crumbling when challenged.
- **Considering other views** — acknowledging that reasonable people disagree (the "argue the other side" skill).
- **Moral maturity** — sitting with a genuinely hard question instead of rushing to a comfortable answer. (Sections D and E especially.)

---

## The three bands (general definitions)

**Strong** — Takes a clear position and gives real reasons. Can make the *opposing* case too, or acknowledge the strongest counter-argument. Holds up under pushback — adjusts thoughtfully rather than collapsing or digging in blindly. Distinguishes fact from opinion, and is honest about what they don't know. Treats a hard question as genuinely hard.

**Developing** — Has a view and some reasons, but the position is thin, or wobbles the moment it's challenged, or they can't articulate the other side without help. Engages willingly; the depth and balance need drawing out with probes.

**Weak** — Hedges indefinitely with no committed view, or asserts an opinion with no reasons, or bluffs knowledge they don't have. Under pushback, either crumbles instantly ("oh yeah, you're right") or repeats the same line louder. Treats a hard moral question as having an obvious, comfortable answer.

---

## How "pushback" and "stuck" prompts work (and how they affect the score)

Two different tools:

- **Pushback** is applied to *every* child, including strong ones — it is the core of the test. Challenge whatever they say, even if you agree with it. Taking the challenge well (engaging, refining, conceding a fair point while holding the core) is **strong evidence**, not a sign they were wrong. Buckling instantly or getting defensive is what marks them down.
- **"If the child is stuck" prompts** are scaffolding for a child who freezes or has no view. Use them sparingly and never hand the child an opinion — offer an angle, a smaller version of the question, or permission not to know. How they use the help is itself evidence.

**Record, per question, both the child's position and how they responded to pushback** — the trajectory under challenge is often the single most predictive signal in the whole interview.

---

## Feedback the model should generate

After the interview, for each question, write **two or three sentences** that:

1. Name **one specific reasoning strength** actually shown (quote or paraphrase the child) — *never* praise or criticise the opinion itself.
2. Name **one specific thing to develop**, framed as a next step ("next time, try making the case for the side you *disagree* with").
3. Stay **warm, concrete, and viewpoint-neutral.**

Avoid generic praise. Tie every comment to something the transcript actually shows.

## SECTION A — Current News & World Affairs

*Start here. The opening question is the most important in the whole interview — it immediately reveals whether the child reads the news and thinks about it.*

---

### A1 — The News Opener *(always ask this first)*

> "Have you been following anything in the news recently? Tell me about it — what's happening, and what do you make of it?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
This opener is the most revealing question in the interview. A strong child names a **specific story** (not just a topic), explains it **accurately in their own words**, and offers a **genuine opinion** — ideally including what they find confusing, surprising, or troubling. The hallmark is cleanly separating **fact** ("what happened") from **view** ("what I make of it"), and saying "I think" when they're giving an opinion. Naming a gap in their own understanding ("I don't fully get why the two sides can't agree") is a plus, not a weakness.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — A specific story, explained accurately, with a reasoned personal view and a clear fact/opinion distinction. Engages with follow-ups, and is honest about the edges of their knowledge.
- **Developing** — Knows of a real story but is vague on the detail, or has facts but only a thin opinion (or an opinion with shaky facts). Comes to life with follow-up prompts.
- **Weak** — Only a broad topic with no actual event ("climate change"), or an opinion presented as fact with no grounding, or "I don't really follow the news" with no attempt to engage.

**Common weak patterns:**
- A topic, not a story ("I've heard about the war") with no specifics.
- Opinion dressed as fact ("the government is just bad") with no event behind it.
- Bluffing a half-remembered headline and inventing detail — worse than admitting uncertainty.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Who do you think is most affected by this story?"*
- *"What do you think should happen next — and who'd disagree with that?"*
- *"Is there anything about this you don't fully understand yet?"* (Reward honest gap-spotting.)
- *"Do you think most people your age know about this? Why or why not?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"It doesn't have to be politics — it could be sport, science, something local, anything you've noticed recently."*
2. *"Have you talked about anything in the news at home or at school lately?"*
3. *"Pick one thing you've heard about, even if you only know a little — tell me what you do know, and what you'd want to find out."*

---

### A2 — One Problem to Solve

> "If you could solve one problem in the world — just one — what would you choose, and why that one above all others?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The hard part isn't *naming* a problem — anyone can — it's **justifying it above all others**. Why *this* one? A strong child names something **specific** ("extreme poverty," not "inequality"), gives a reason it beats the other candidates (most urgent? most foundational, so solving it unlocks others? most solvable?), acknowledges a **trade-off** ("if I poured everything here, something else gets less"), and shows some grasp of *why it hasn't been solved already*. The reasoning behind the choice matters far more than which problem they pick.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Specific problem, with a genuine argument for *why this one above others*, plus awareness of trade-offs or why it's unsolved. Holds the comparison in mind, not just the single choice.
- **Developing** — Names a real, specific problem and one reason for caring, but treats "why this one rather than another" as obvious and doesn't really compare. Engages when pushed to compare.
- **Weak** — A vague category ("badness," "war") with no reason for the choice, or can't say why their problem matters more than any other. No sense of prioritisation.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Naming a problem but giving no reason for choosing *it* over others — missing the whole point of "just one."
- Picking something so broad it isn't actionable ("sadness," "evil").
- Assuming their solution is easy and ignoring why the problem persists.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Why that one rather than [a big problem they didn't mention — disease, climate, war]?"*
- *"Who would resist your solution, and why might they have a point?"*
- *"If you fixed that, would other problems get easier — or is it separate?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about problems that affect lots of people — health, poverty, the environment, fairness. Any of those pull at you?"*
2. *"Is there a problem you'd most hate to still exist when you're grown up?"*
3. *"Pick any one to start with — we can always change it — and tell me why it matters to you."*

---

### A3 — Richer Countries & Poorer Countries

> "Do you think wealthy countries have a responsibility to help poorer ones — or should each country focus on its own people first?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The genuine tension is **national interest vs. global solidarity**, and both sides have serious arguments. A strong child takes a side *and shows they understand the other*:
- **For helping:** global inequality is partly the result of history (colonialism, trade systems); extreme poverty causes preventable deaths; in a connected world, problems don't stay contained.
- **Against an obligation:** governments are elected to serve their *own* citizens first; aid can create dependency; "charity begins at home."
The hallmark is "I think they should help, *but I can see why people disagree, because…*" — treating it as a real dilemma, not a question with an obvious answer.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Commits to a position, gives reasons, *and* articulates the strongest version of the opposing view. Engages with the moral-vs-legal and luck-vs-government nuances when probed.
- **Developing** — Takes a side with a reason, but treats the other side as obviously wrong or can only caricature it. Acknowledges complexity when prompted.
- **Weak** — "Of course they should help" / "no, look after your own" with no reasons, and no sense that a thoughtful person could disagree.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Treating it as obvious in either direction, with no acknowledgement of the other side.
- Pure emotional appeal ("poor people are sad") with no argument about *responsibility*.
- Not distinguishing "it would be *kind* to help" from "they have a *duty* to help."

**Live probes / pushback:**
- (If they say help:) *"But a government is elected by its own people, with its own poor to look after — why should they send money abroad?"*
- (If they say don't:) *"If a country got rich partly through its history with poorer ones, does that change things?"*
- *"Does it matter whether the country is poor through bad luck or bad government?"*
- *"Is there a difference between a *moral* responsibility and a *legal* one?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think of it like people: if you have plenty and your neighbour has nothing, do you owe them anything?"*
2. *"What's one reason a country *should* help — and one reason it might focus on its own people?"*
3. *"You don't have to be certain — just lean one way for now and tell me why."*

---

### A4 — Is It Ever Right to Break the Law?

> "Can you think of a situation where breaking the law might actually be the right thing to do?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
Most children can reach "yes, sometimes" with a **historical example** — hiding people from the Nazis, the suffragettes, Rosa Parks, helping an enslaved person escape. The richer thinking is in the *deeper question*: what makes a law unjust? Who decides? If anyone who *thought* a law was wrong could just break it, could society function at all? A strong child both gives a concrete example *and* grapples with where the line sits — ideally distinguishing a law that is **morally wrong** from one they simply find inconvenient.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Gives a genuine example of a justified law-break *and* engages with the limit: how unjust must a law be, who decides, and why "I disagree with it" isn't enough on its own. Distinguishes unjust laws from merely disliked ones.
- **Developing** — Reaches "it depends on the law" with an example, but doesn't probe where the line is or who decides, until pushed. Starting point of "always obey" is fine if they move.
- **Weak** — "You must always follow the law, full stop" with no flexibility even when offered an extreme case, or "break any law you don't like" with no limiting principle.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Treating all laws as equally sacred, even when given the hiding-from-the-Nazis case.
- The opposite: "if it's unfair, just break it," with no worry about who judges fairness.
- Confusing *disagreeing* with a law with the law being *unjust*.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"So where's the line? How unfair does a law have to be before breaking it is right?"*
- *"What if two people disagree about whether a law is unjust — say someone thinks a tax is unfair and refuses to pay? Are they justified too?"*
- *"Is there a difference between breaking a law and working to change it?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think back in history — have there been laws we'd now all agree were wrong?"*
2. *"Imagine a law that told you to do something clearly cruel. Would you have to obey it?"*
3. *"Start simple: are *all* laws always right, or could a law itself be wrong? Give me your gut feeling and we'll build from there."*

---

### A5 — Britain and the Wider World

> "Which country, or part of the world, do you think Britain has its most important relationship with right now — and why that one?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
This is the most **knowledge-dependent** question in the section, so calibrate expectations: genuine awareness matters, but reasoning still counts most. Plausible answers include the **US** (military alliance, cultural ties, trade), **Europe** (proximity, shared history, the post-Brexit relationship), **Ukraine/Russia** (conflict on Europe's doorstep), **India or China** (trade, geopolitics), or the **Commonwealth**. The strongest answers show that relationships are **complicated** — "the US is our closest ally, but we don't always agree"; "China is a huge trade partner *and* a political rival." Honest uncertainty ("I'm not sure, but I'd guess…") beats confident bluffing.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Names a relationship and gives a real reason, ideally showing it's not simple (ally-but-tensions, partner-but-rival). Honest about the limits of what they know.
- **Developing** — Names a sensible country with a basic reason ("the US, because they're our friends"), but one-dimensional, and thin on recent events. Builds with prompts.
- **Weak** — Can't name any relationship, or bluffs with invented detail, or picks somewhere with no reason at all. Confident-but-empty.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Bluffing — confidently asserting something made-up rather than admitting uncertainty.
- A flat "they're our friends" with no sense of trade, security, or history.
- Treating a relationship as purely good or purely bad, missing the mixed reality.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Has anything happened recently that affected that relationship?"*
- *"You said they're an ally — is it all straightforward, or are there tensions too?"*
- *"Do you think Britain has more or less influence in the world than it did 20 years ago?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about countries Britain trades with, or has a history with, or relies on for security."*
2. *"Is there a country that comes up a lot in the news in connection with Britain?"*
3. *"You don't need to be certain — make your best guess and tell me why you'd pick that one."*

## SECTION B — Technology & AI

*AI is the defining topic of this generation's childhood. Every top school will touch on it.*

---

### B1 — Excited or Worried About AI?

> "Artificial intelligence is developing very fast. Is that something you feel excited about, worried about — or both? What's your honest view?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
No right answer — what's prized is **nuance**, holding two things at once. A strong child can supply reasons on **both** sides and lands on "I feel both, and here's why":
- **For excitement:** medical breakthroughs, solving hard problems, freeing people from drudgery, accessibility tools, creative help.
- **For worry:** job loss, misinformation, privacy, over-dependence, autonomous weapons, AI outrunning human control.
The hallmark is genuine tension, not a one-word verdict. "I'm not worried at all" or "I'm terrified," with no qualification, both miss the complexity.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Holds excitement *and* worry together, with a concrete reason for each, and maybe a sense of which worries are nearer-term vs. speculative. Comfortable with the ambivalence.
- **Developing** — Leans one way with a reason, and can produce the other side when asked. Some nuance, but it needs drawing out.
- **Weak** — All-in on one side with no reasons ("robots are cool" / "AI is scary"), and can't generate the opposing consideration even when prompted.

**Common weak patterns:**
- A single undefended feeling with no specifics.
- Sci-fi worries (killer robots) with no everyday ones (misinformation, jobs), or vice versa.
- Treating "both" as a dodge ("a bit of both") with no reasons attached.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- (If excited:) *"What about people whose jobs disappear, or being unable to tell what's real online — does that not worry you?"*
- (If worried:) *"But AI is already spotting diseases earlier than doctors can — isn't that worth a lot?"*
- *"Is there a specific use of AI that worries you more than the others?"*
- *"Should AI ever decide things that change people's lives — who gets a job, or a prison sentence?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think of one good thing AI could do, and one thing that could go wrong."*
2. *"Have you used anything with AI in it? How did it feel — useful, strange, a bit of both?"*
3. *"You don't have to pick a side — just tell me one thing that excites you and one that gives you pause."*

---

### B2 — AI and Cheating

> "If a student used AI to help them write an essay — not copy it entirely, but get help with ideas and structure — would you consider that cheating? Where do you draw the line?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The richest move is to see a **spectrum** rather than a yes/no: AI checking spelling → suggesting vocabulary → restructuring an argument → generating the argument → writing the whole thing. *Where* on that line does it become cheating, and why there? The very best answers reframe the question around **what learning is for** — "it depends what the teacher was trying to test." A child who notices that the essay might be there to assess *thinking* (not just to produce a document) is reasoning well beyond rule-following. Honesty about their *own* AI use is a bonus schools value.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Draws a line *and justifies where* by appealing to purpose (what's being assessed, whether the student still does the thinking). Handles the dictionary/thesaurus/Google comparison thoughtfully. May be candid about their own use.
- **Developing** — Has a sensible instinct ("a bit of help is fine, writing it all isn't") but draws the line by feel, and only engages with *why* when pushed. Some grasp of the spectrum.
- **Weak** — A flat rule ("any AI is cheating" / "it's fine, everyone does it") with no line-drawing and no thought about purpose. Pure rule-following or pure dismissal.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Binary verdict with no spectrum and no "why there."
- Ignoring the question of *what the essay is for*.
- Inconsistency they don't notice (a thesaurus is fine but AI vocabulary isn't — why?).

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Is using a dictionary cheating? A thesaurus? Google? What's different about AI?"*
- *"If the student fully understood what the AI produced and could defend every line, does that change it?"*
- *"What's the essay actually *for* — to test the student's thinking, or just to hand in some writing?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Imagine a ladder: at the bottom, AI fixes a spelling mistake; at the top, it writes the whole essay. Where would *you* put the cheating line?"*
2. *"Think about why teachers set essays in the first place — what are they trying to find out?"*
3. *"Start with an easy case: is spell-check cheating? Now move up a step and see when it starts to feel wrong."*

---

### B3 — The Creativity Question

> "Do you think a computer could ever be truly creative — like write a poem that genuinely means something, or paint a picture that moves people? Or is creativity something only humans can do?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The single best move is to **define "creative" before answering** — this question rewards the child who says "it depends what you mean by creative." Both sides have force:
- **Computers can be creative:** AI already makes art, music and poetry people find moving; maybe creativity *is* just recombining old ideas in new ways — which machines do.
- **They can't:** real creativity needs experience, emotion, intention, a wish to *communicate* something — and the AI doesn't know *why* it made what it made.
The deepest version asks: is creativity in the **process** or the **output**? If a poem moves you, does it matter who — or what — wrote it?

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Pauses to pin down what "creative" means, then argues from that definition, engaging both the output and the process/intention angles. Comfortable that the answer turns on the definition.
- **Developing** — Takes a side with a reason (often "no, machines don't have feelings") but treats "creative" as obvious and doesn't examine it until prompted. Some depth available.
- **Weak** — Flat verdict ("computers can't think so no" / "AI makes pictures so yes") with no attempt to say what creativity *is*. No engagement with the deeper question.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Answering as if "creative" has one obvious meaning.
- Judging only the *output* ("the picture looks nice, so it's creative") or only the *intention*, never noticing the two can come apart.
- "Computers can't have feelings" stated as a conversation-ender, not a starting point.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Before you decide — what do you actually mean by 'creative'? Is a machine that makes a beautiful image creative, or only if it *meant* something by it?"*
- *"If a poem genuinely moves you, does it matter whether a person or a machine wrote it?"*
- *"Is creativity about the thing that's made, or about what's going on inside the maker?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Start with people: what makes something a *person* does count as creative?"*
2. *"Now ask whether a computer could have that same thing — and which part it might be missing."*
3. *"You could split it: maybe a computer can make something that *looks* creative, but is that the same as *being* creative? What do you think?"*

---

### B4 — The Job a Robot Could Never Do

> "Is there a job you think a robot or AI could never do — not just 'couldn't do yet', but genuinely could never do, no matter how advanced technology gets? Why?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The key distinction is **"currently difficult" vs. "genuinely impossible forever."** Children often name teacher, doctor, artist, counsellor — and these are exactly the ones to **push back on**, because AI already tutors, assists in surgery, makes art, and delivers therapy. The harder-to-dismiss answers point at something other than capability: roles needing genuine human **presence and relationship** (a parent, a friend), or genuine **moral responsibility** (a judge — *can a machine be accountable for a decision?*). A strong child realises the real issue may be **accountability or relationship**, not skill.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Distinguishes "can't do *yet*" from "can never do," survives the pushback on obvious jobs, and locates the sticking point in something deeper — accountability, genuine relationship, being held responsible. Philosophical, not just practical.
- **Developing** — Names a role and a reason, but when shown AI can already do a version of it, has to think hard; with prompting, moves toward the presence/responsibility idea. Good raw instinct.
- **Weak** — Names a job ("doctor") and, when told AI assists in surgery, simply switches to another with the same flaw, never reaching the "what's *really* different about humans" question.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Choosing a job AI demonstrably already helps with, and treating "a robot couldn't do that" as obvious.
- Confusing "I wouldn't *want* a robot to" with "a robot *couldn't*."
- Never moving past capability to ask about responsibility or relationship.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- (Whatever they pick:) *"Actually, AI already does a version of that — it tutors, assists surgeons, makes art, even gives therapy. So is it really impossible, or just early days?"*
- *"Would you be okay with a machine *being responsible* for a decision — say, a judge sending someone to prison?"*
- *"Is the problem that the machine *can't* do it, or that we wouldn't accept it doing it?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about jobs that are less about a skill and more about *being a person with* someone."*
2. *"Is there a job where, even if a machine did it perfectly, something important would still be missing?"*
3. *"Try one and test it: could a machine ever really do this — and if not, what exactly is it lacking?"*

## SECTION C — Climate & The Environment

---

### C1 — Who Should Act?

> "Who do you think has the biggest responsibility to tackle climate change — governments, businesses, or individual people? You have to pick one."

**What a strong answer involves:**
Forcing the pick matters — "everyone" is the easy dodge. The real question is about **leverage and accountability**, and each option has a genuine case:
- **Government:** only it can set *binding* rules and systemic change; individuals alone can't move the needle; businesses respond to regulation.
- **Business:** corporations produce most emissions, hold the resources and technology, and their lobbying often blocks change.
- **Individuals:** consumer choices collectively steer business; democratic pressure forces government to act; behaviour sets cultural norms.
A strong child **commits to one but acknowledges the other two**, and the very best note that the three are **connected** (governments are pushed by voters, businesses respond to both) — systems thinking — *before* still making the choice.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Picks one and gives a leverage-based reason, having briefly weighed the others. May spot the interconnection while still committing. Holds the choice under pushback.
- **Developing** — Picks one with a reason, but doesn't really weigh the alternatives until prompted. Sensible but one-dimensional.
- **Weak** — Refuses to choose ("everyone's responsible") and won't commit even when pressed, or names one with no reason at all.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Dodging the forced choice with "it's everyone."
- Naming a culprit with no account of *why it has the most leverage*.
- Blaming a group emotionally ("greedy companies") without an argument about responsibility or power.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- (If government:) *"But governments are slow and worried about votes — can you really rely on them?"*
- (If individuals:) *"Some say focusing on individuals — guilt over plastic straws — is a distraction that lets big polluters off the hook. Fair?"*
- (If business:) *"Businesses mostly do what customers and laws push them to — so aren't you really pointing back at us or the government?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about who has the *power* to make the biggest change — and who *causes* the most of the problem. Are they the same?"*
2. *"If you had to bet on one of the three actually making a difference, which would it be?"*
3. *"Pick one for now, even if it's close — and tell me why you leaned that way."*

---

### C2 — Intergenerational Fairness

> "Is it fair that your generation has to deal with a problem — climate change — that it didn't really create?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
By most definitions this *is* unfair — but the real test is whether the child keeps two questions apart: **"is it fair?"** and **"do we still have to act?"** A strong child says something like *"No, it isn't fair — but that doesn't mean we get to ignore it."* They engage the genuine tensions:
- Whether it's fair is **separate** from whether it must be dealt with.
- This generation inherits **benefits** too (medicine, technology, relative peace), not only problems.
- "Creating" is slippery — earlier generations may not have understood the consequences.
The standout move is refusing to slide from "it's not fair" to "so it's not our problem."

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Cleanly separates fairness from responsibility ("unfair, but we still must act"), and engages at least one deeper tension (inherited benefits, or what "creating" really means). Doesn't let the unfairness become an excuse.
- **Developing** — Says it's unfair with a reason, but needs prompting to see that unfairness doesn't cancel the duty to act. Reasons clearly once the two questions are separated for them.
- **Weak** — "It's not fair, so it's not really our job" stated as obvious — the key logical error — with no awareness it's even contestable; or just "yeah it's unfair" with nothing more.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Sliding from "unfair" straight to "therefore not our responsibility."
- Only seeing inherited problems, never the inherited benefits.
- Treating "fair" as simple, with no thought about what fairness even requires here.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Suppose I agree it's unfair. Does that actually let your generation off the hook?"*
- *"Your generation also inherits medicine, technology, peace — does that change the fairness picture?"*
- *"Did the people who caused it know what they were doing? Does that matter for blame?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Try splitting it into two questions: first, is it fair? Then separately, should we still do something about it?"*
2. *"Think of inheriting a messy room you didn't make — is it fair, and does someone still have to tidy it?"*
3. *"Give me your gut on the fairness part first, and we'll take the 'what now' part after."*

---

### C3 — Personal Sacrifice

> "Is there something you would genuinely be willing to give up — in your own life — to help the environment? Not something easy. Something that would actually cost you."

**What a strong answer involves:**
This question is about **honesty and self-reflection**, not extracting a particular sacrifice. The prize is a child who is candid about the gap between what they think they **should** do and what they think they **actually would** do — "honestly, I'd find it really hard to give up meat, but I think I should." That beats a glib "I'd give up flying" from a child who clearly hasn't pictured what that costs. **Moral self-awareness** is the quality being tested, not moral self-congratulation.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Names a *genuine* cost to themselves and is honest about how hard it would be, including any gap between their ideals and their likely behaviour. Reflective rather than performative.
- **Developing** — Offers a real sacrifice but a fairly painless one, or claims an easy willingness without much reflection. Becomes more honest when gently pressed.
- **Weak** — Either names something costless ("I'd recycle more") as if it were a sacrifice, or performs virtue ("I'd give up everything!") with no sign they've thought about the cost.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Choosing something that costs them nothing and calling it a sacrifice.
- Performing the "good" answer rather than being honest.
- Naming a big sacrifice with no apparent grasp of what it would actually mean day to day.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Be honest — would you *actually* do that, or is it more what you feel you *should* say?"*
- *"Is it fair to ask people to give things up when the big systems around them aren't changing?"*
- *"What would be the hardest part of that, really?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about your actual day — food, travel, things you buy, screen time. Which would genuinely be hard to change?"*
2. *"It's okay to say something would be difficult — I'm more interested in honesty than in a heroic answer."*
3. *"Name one thing you'd find hard to give up, and just tell me why it'd be hard."*

## SECTION D — Moral Dilemmas

*These questions have no right answer. The goal is to see how the child reasons through genuine ethical tension.*

---

### D1 — Five Strangers or One Friend

> "Imagine you're in a situation where you could save five strangers from serious harm, or save one person who is your closest friend — but not both. What would you do?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
This is a child-friendly **trolley problem**, pitting **relationships against numbers**. Both sides are serious:
- **Save the friend:** loyalty and love matter; we can't be purely calculating machines; the specific bonds we have carry real moral weight.
- **Save the five:** numbers count ethically; your friend would probably *want* you to save five; treating everyone impartially is a basic moral demand.
What's actually being scored is whether the child **feels the difficulty**. "Obviously my friend, no question" has dodged the dilemma; "I'd probably save my friend, but I know that's not the most logical answer" shows real moral honesty — holding what they'd *do* against what they think is *right*.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Recognises it as genuinely hard, gives reasons on both sides, takes a position, and is honest about any gap between instinct and logic. Stays thoughtful as the numbers/details change.
- **Developing** — Picks a side with a reason, but treats it as easy; with pushback (the elderly version, the fifty version) starts to feel the tension. Engages once nudged.
- **Weak** — "Obviously my friend" or "obviously the five" with no sense that the other side has any force, and unmoved even when the scenario is varied.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Declaring it obvious in either direction — the main thing to challenge.
- Going coldly utilitarian ("five is more than one, done") with no feel for the loyalty pull, or the reverse.
- Trying to escape the dilemma ("I'd save everyone somehow") rather than choosing.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Would it change if the five strangers were very old and your friend was young?"*
- *"What if it were fifty strangers instead of five? Five hundred?"*
- (If they chose the five:) *"But this is your closest friend — can we really expect someone to be that impartial about people they love?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"There's no right answer here — just tell me which way you lean and we'll explore it."*
2. *"What does your gut say first? Now — is there anything to be said for the other choice?"*
3. *"Imagine you had to look either your friend, or the five families, in the eye afterwards. Does that help you feel which way you'd go?"*

---

### D2 — When Lying is Right

> "Can you think of a situation where lying might actually be the morally correct thing to do?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
Most children can reach "yes, sometimes" with a **concrete example** — lying to an aggressor about where someone is hiding, a white lie to spare needless hurt, protecting a surprise. The richer thinking is the **limit problem**: if lying is *sometimes* right, what stops that reasoning excusing *any* lie? A strong child gives a specific case **and** names the **principle** that made the lie acceptable — compassion, preventing serious harm, protecting someone's dignity — rather than just "it was nice."

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Offers a specific, vivid scenario *and* articulates the underlying principle, then engages with where the limit sits (why this lie is okay but a selfish lie isn't). Distinguishes protecting-someone from protecting-myself.
- **Developing** — Gives a real example and senses it's justified, but explains it only as "to be kind," and needs prompting to reach for the principle or the limit. Sound instinct.
- **Weak** — Either "lying is always wrong, no exceptions" with no flexibility even given the aggressor case, or "lying's fine if it helps you" with no limit at all.

**Common weak patterns:**
- A vague scenario ("if it helps someone") with nothing concrete.
- Justifying with "it's nice" rather than a principle (harm, dignity, compassion).
- No worry about the slippery slope from "some lies" to "any lie I find convenient."

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"What exactly made *that* lie okay? Could that same reason be used to excuse a selfish lie?"*
- *"Where's the limit — what's a lie that crosses the line, and why?"*
- *"Is there a difference between lying to protect someone else and lying to protect yourself?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Imagine someone dangerous asks where your friend is hiding. Would telling the truth be the right thing?"*
2. *"Think of a time a small lie might spare someone real, pointless hurt."*
3. *"Give me one situation where a lie might be kind — then we'll work out what made it okay."*

---

### D3 — The Best Friend's Cheating

> "You find out that your closest friend cheated in an important school exam. Not a small thing — it's the kind of exam that affects their future. Do you say something, and if so, to whom?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
A three-way conflict: **loyalty** to the friend, **fairness** to everyone who didn't cheat, and the friend's own **long-term interest**. There's a spread of options — say nothing (and own why), talk to the friend privately and urge them to come clean, go straight to a teacher, or do nothing but feel bad. A strong child weighs the **consequences** of options, not just abstract principle, and often finds the nuanced middle: *"I'd talk to my friend first and push them to own up themselves"* — protecting the friendship while still acting ethically. The key test comes in the follow-up, when the friend refuses.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Names the competing pulls, considers more than one course of action and its consequences, and reasons toward a defensible plan. Holds up (adapts thoughtfully) when the friend refuses to come clean.
- **Developing** — Picks a reasonable action with a reason, but sees it one-dimensionally (just loyalty, or just rules) until the other pulls are raised. Engages with the follow-up.
- **Weak** — A reflex with no weighing — "I'd never tell on a friend, ever" or "I'd report them instantly" — and no movement when the stakes or the friend's refusal are introduced.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Seeing only loyalty *or* only the rule, never the tension between them.
- Ignoring what's actually best for the friend in the long run.
- No thought about *consequences* — treating it as a slogan rather than a situation.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"What if your friend said they won't tell anyone — and begs you not to either?"*
- (If they'd report:) *"Is there nothing owed to a close friend? Could you have handled it more gently first?"*
- (If they'd stay silent:) *"Is that fair on everyone who *didn't* cheat — and is it even good for your friend?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Notice the pull in different directions: your friend, the other students, and your friend's own future. Which tugs hardest?"*
2. *"There's a whole range — from saying nothing, to talking to your friend, to telling a teacher. Where do you land?"*
3. *"Just tell me your first instinct, and then we'll test it together."*

---

### D4 — Motive vs. Outcome

> "A very wealthy person donates a huge amount of money to build a hospital in a poor area. But they only do it because it makes them look good — it's purely for the publicity. Is it still a good thing to do? Does their motive matter?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
Two moral traditions pull against each other here:
- **Outcome view (consequentialism):** what matters is the result — the hospital exists, lives are saved — so the motive is irrelevant.
- **Motive view (virtue/duty ethics):** part of what makes an act *good* is *why* it was done; a good deed done for selfish reasons is morally compromised.
The standout move is separating **"is it a good outcome?"** from **"is it a good act / a good person?"** — a child who says *"The outcome is genuinely good, but we shouldn't praise them as if they were being kind, because they weren't"* has nailed the distinction.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Explicitly splits outcome from motive, can argue each, and reaches a position that honours both (good result, but not praiseworthy *as character*). Handles the anonymous-donation twist.
- **Developing** — Takes a side ("still good, the hospital helps people" or "no, they're selfish") with a reason, but treats outcome and motive as the same question until they're prised apart. Gets there with prompting.
- **Weak** — A flat verdict — "good, it's a hospital" or "bad, they're selfish" — with no sense that outcome and motive are two different things.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Collapsing the two questions into one ("it helped people so the person is good").
- Judging only the motive and ignoring the very real benefit, or vice versa.
- No vocabulary for "good thing happened" vs. "good person did it."

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Does it change if the donation was anonymous — no credit at all? Why does that shift things?"*
- *"Should we care about people's *motives*, or only about what they actually do?"*
- (If "still good":) *"So should we praise them just as much as someone who gave quietly out of kindness?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Try two separate questions: is the *result* good? And is the *person* good for doing it that way?"*
2. *"Picture two donors — one does it to look good, one does it secretly out of kindness. Same act, same hospital. Any difference?"*
3. *"Give me your gut: good thing, bad thing, or a bit of both — and we'll unpick why."*

---

### D5 — The Found Money

> "You find a £50 note on the pavement in a busy high street. There's no one obviously nearby who might have dropped it. What do you do — and be honest?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
The **honest** version is harder than the **virtuous** one — and honesty is the point. The aim isn't to extract "I'd hand it to the police"; it's to see whether the child engages with the real tension between what they think they **should** do and what they think they **would** do. It helps to acknowledge the messy reality (police are unlikely to find the owner; "finders-keepers" is a common norm; the owner may never be found). A child who says *"I'd probably keep it, but feel a bit guilty — and here's how I'd justify it to myself"* is being far more reflective than one who performs "I'd hand it in, obviously" with no hesitation.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Honest about what they'd really do *and* why, engages the should/would gap, and can reason about the competing considerations (the owner, the odds, the norm) rather than reciting the "correct" answer.
- **Developing** — Gives a sincere-sounding answer with a reason, but doesn't probe the should/would gap until prompted. Some genuine reflection.
- **Weak** — A polished "hand it to the police, obviously" with no engagement (likely performance), or "keep it, finders-keepers" with no acknowledgement that there's any tension at all.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Performing virtue — the textbook answer with no sign of real reflection.
- The opposite: keeping it with zero acknowledgement of the owner's loss.
- Not noticing that "what I should do" and "what I'd actually do" might differ.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Be honest — is that what you'd *actually* do, or what you feel you *should* say?"*
- *"Do you think most people your age would do the same? Does what others would do change what's right?"*
- (If hand it in:) *"The police almost certainly won't find the owner — does that change anything for you?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"There's no trap here — I'm genuinely more interested in honesty than in the 'good' answer."*
2. *"Think about two things separately: what's the right thing to do, and what you think you'd really do in the moment."*
3. *"Picture it actually happening — £50 on the pavement, no one around. What's your first instinct?"*

## SECTION E — Society, Rights & Fairness

---

### E1 — Inequality of Birth

> "Is it fair that some people are born into wealthy families while others are born into poverty — and that this affects almost every opportunity they'll have in life?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
"No, it's not fair" is the easy opening — the thinking is in what comes next. A strong child engages the real tensions:
- What would a genuinely **fair alternative** even look like? Redistribute wealth at birth? Abolish inheritance? Each has its own problems.
- Is **luck** ever "fair" or "unfair," or is fairness only about things we *control*?
- Does the unfairness of birth create an **obligation to compensate** — and if so, *who* compensates, and how?
The best answers don't stop at "it's wrong"; they ask what could realistically be done, and notice that "unfair" and "fixable" are not the same thing.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Goes beyond "unfair" to grapple with what a fair alternative would be and whether/how it could work, distinguishing "this is unfair" from "this can or should be changed." Engages the provocative self-referential probe with composure.
- **Developing** — Says it's unfair with a reason and can discuss *some* remedy when prompted, but initially treats "unfair → just fix it" as simple. Thoughtful once pushed.
- **Weak** — "It's not fair" and nothing more, or insists on a fix without any thought about what it would cost or whether it's possible. Deflects the school-fees probe.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Equating "unfair" with "easily fixable," ignoring the difficulty of any alternative.
- Strong feeling, no constructive thought about remedies or trade-offs.
- Dodging the uncomfortable point that they're sitting in a fee-charging school.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"What would a *fair* version actually look like — and would that bring its own problems?"*
- *"You're at an interview for a school that charges fees most families can't afford. Is that relevant here?"* (Deliberately provocative — reward engagement over deflection.)
- *"Is something automatically wrong just because it's down to luck, or only if we could have changed it?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Start simple — does it feel fair to you? Then we'll ask the harder question of what could be done about it."*
2. *"Think about what's in a child's control when they're born. Does that matter for fairness?"*
3. *"Imagine you could change one thing to make it fairer — what would you try, and what might go wrong?"*

---

### E2 — Should 16-Year-Olds Vote?

> "Should the voting age be lowered to 16? What's your view — and make the case for the other side too."

**What a strong answer involves:**
The question *explicitly asks for both sides*, so it directly tests **arguing the case you disagree with** — a core intellectual-fairness skill.
- **For lowering:** 16-year-olds pay taxes and can (with consent) join the army; they'll live longest with today's decisions; Scotland let 16-year-olds vote in its independence referendum; civic habits should start early.
- **Against:** brain development (impulse control, long-term thinking) continues into the mid-20s; many 16-year-olds still live with and echo their parents; 18 is a long-standing, broadly respected line.
A strong child gives a clear view *and* makes the opposing case with real force — and the best notice they personally have a **stake** in the answer.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — States a view *and* builds a genuinely fair version of the other side (not a straw man). May note their own stake. Even-handed and specific.
- **Developing** — Has a clear view with reasons, but the opposing case is thin or reluctant, improving when pushed. Willing but unbalanced.
- **Weak** — Only argues their own side, or makes a deliberately weak version of the other ("some people are just against young people"). Can't genuinely inhabit the disagreement.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Refusing or failing to make the opposing case with any real strength.
- Straw-manning the other side rather than steelmanning it.
- Self-interest unexamined ("yes, because I'd like to vote") with no broader reasoning.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Now give me the *strongest* version of the side you don't agree with."*
- *"You're close to this age yourself — does that make your answer more or less reliable, do you think?"*
- (Whichever side:) *"What's the best reason a thoughtful person could give for the opposite?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Start with what you think — should it be 16 or stay at 18? — and one reason why."*
2. *"Now pretend you have to convince someone of the *opposite*. What would you say?"*
3. *"Think about what 16-year-olds can and can't already do — work, taxes, driving — does that help you weigh it?"*

---

### E3 — Social Media: Good or Harm?

> "Do you think social media does more good or more harm for people your age? You've grown up with it — I want your honest view."

**What a strong answer involves:**
The child has **lived experience** of this, and the strongest answers draw on it — "I've noticed in myself that…" is exactly the **self-reflection** top schools treasure. Both sides are real:
- **For harm:** mental-health effects (comparison, cyberbullying, lost sleep); misinformation; addiction *by design*; crowding out real-world time.
- **For good:** connecting isolated young people; organising and activism; creative expression; access to information and communities; a wider view of the world.
A standout answer notices that tech companies **design** products to maximise engagement — which complicates the idea that it's all just "the user's choice."

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Weighs both sides, draws on genuine personal observation, and grasps the design/engagement point (so "just choose to use it less" is too simple). Reaches a considered view.
- **Developing** — Has a view with reasons, mostly abstract, and acknowledges the other side when prompted. Personal reflection appears with encouragement.
- **Weak** — One-sided ("it's just bad" / "it's fine") with no personal insight and no awareness that the products are engineered to be compelling.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Only abstract talking points, no lived reflection — or pure anecdote with no broader view.
- Treating usage as purely free choice, ignoring deliberate addictive design.
- A flat verdict with no weighing of the genuine benefits.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Should there be a minimum age to own a smartphone? What age, and why?"*
- *"If it's harmful, whose fault is it — the companies, the parents, or the users themselves?"*
- *"You say people should just use it less — but if it's *designed* to be hard to put down, is that really a fair ask?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Think about your own experience — when has it felt good, and when has it felt bad?"*
2. *"Name one way it helps people your age, and one way it might harm them."*
3. *"On balance, if you had to lean one way — more good or more harm — which, and why?"*

---

### E4 — Animals and Rights

> "Do you think animals should have rights — and if so, which ones? Are some animals more deserving of rights than others?"

**What a strong answer involves:**
Like the "creativity" question, the sharpest move is to ask **what a "right" actually is** before deciding whether animals can have them — a right implies a being with **interests that can be violated**, which leads straight to consciousness and sentience. A strong child opens the idea up (a right not to suffer needlessly; not to be killed for trivial reasons; to live in conditions suited to its nature) and then engages the hard edges:
- Do *all* animals have equal standing, or does it depend on intelligence/sentience?
- If a cow has a right not to suffer, what does that imply about eating meat?
- Where's the line — insects? bacteria?
"It depends what you mean by a right" is exactly the right starting instinct.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Pauses on what a "right" requires (a being that can suffer or have interests), then reasons about which animals qualify and follows the awkward implications (meat, where to draw the line) honestly.
- **Developing** — Says animals should have *some* rights with a sensible reason (don't cause needless pain), but treats "right" as obvious and needs prompting toward sentience and the harder edges.
- **Weak** — A flat "yes animals have rights" / "no they don't" with no thought about what a right is or which animals, and no engagement with the consequences.

**Common weak patterns:**
- Not examining what "having a right" even means.
- Asserting rights for cute or familiar animals only, with no principle for where the line falls.
- Avoiding the uncomfortable follow-through (e.g. the implications for eating meat).

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"Before you decide — what does it even mean to *have* a right? Does a being need to be able to suffer, or to want things?"*
- *"If a cow has a right not to suffer, what does that say about eating meat?"*
- *"Where's the line — a dog? a fish? an insect? bacteria? Why there?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"Start with people: why do *we* have rights? Now ask which of those reasons might apply to animals."*
2. *"Is there a difference between a dog and a worm when it comes to this? What's the difference that matters?"*
3. *"Pick one right you think an animal should have, and tell me why."*

---

### E5 — School Uniforms

> "Do you think school uniforms are a good idea? Make the strongest case for the opposite of whatever you actually believe."

**What a strong answer involves:**
The twist — *argue against your own instinct* — is the whole point. This is the purest test of **steelmanning**: building the strongest possible case for a view you don't hold. The material is rich both ways:
- **For uniforms:** reduces visible inequality; cuts distraction; builds identity and belonging; cheaper overall than a wardrobe.
- **Against uniforms:** suppresses individuality; doesn't really remove inequality (shoes, bags, phones remain); compliance isn't the same as good values; can enforce conformity over character.
The gold standard is a child who argues the *other* side with genuine force — and the very best say *"actually, having argued the other side, I'm less sure than I was."* That shift is exactly the habit top schools want to grow.

**Scoring rubric:**

- **Strong** — Builds a genuinely strong case *against their own view*, not a token one — and may honestly report that doing so softened their certainty. Intellectually fair and self-aware.
- **Developing** — Manages a counter-argument but a thin or reluctant one, needing encouragement to make it as strong as it could be. Willing, not yet fluent.
- **Weak** — Can't or won't leave their own view, or offers a two-sentence straw man before snapping back to what they actually think. No real steelmanning.

**Common weak patterns:**
- A grudging, deliberately weak version of the opposing case.
- Sliding back into their own opinion instead of staying in the other side's shoes.
- Treating "argue the other side" as a trick rather than a genuine exercise.

**Live probes / pushback:**
- *"That was the case against your view — but make it *stronger*. What's the best point on that side?"*
- *"Having argued the other side, are you any less certain than when you started?"*
- *"If a clever friend disagreed with you, what's the one argument of theirs you'd find hardest to answer?"*

**If the child is stuck:**
1. *"First, just tell me what *you* actually think about uniforms."*
2. *"Now the hard bit — pretend you have to convince me of the opposite. Give me your best go."*
3. *"Think about someone who'd really disagree with you — a parent, a friend — what would *they* say?"*

## End-of-interview synthesis

After the discussion questions, the model produces an **overall profile** of *how this child thinks and reasons* — never a tally of "good" opinions. Summarise across the five qualities, citing where each appeared:

- **World awareness** — mainly A1, A5 (and any current-affairs detail elsewhere). Genuine engagement with the world, honestly calibrated.
- **Forming an opinion** — everywhere a position is asked for (A2, A3, C1, E2). Do they commit, or hedge forever?
- **Defending a position** — every question with pushback. Do they hold up and refine, or crumble / dig in?
- **Considering other views** — A3, B-section, E2, E5 especially. Can they make the opposing case in good faith?
- **Moral maturity** — Sections D and E. Can they sit with a hard question instead of grabbing the comfortable answer?

**Recommended overall shape (per child):**
1. A two-to-three sentence **narrative** of their thinking style ("Forms views quickly and defends them well, but tends to dismiss the other side — strongest when forced to argue against himself, which visibly made him more thoughtful").
2. **Two genuine strengths**, each tied to a specific moment in the transcript.
3. **One or two growth areas**, framed as next steps ("practise making the opposing case as strong as your own").
4. A note on **trajectory under pushback** — did challenge sharpen or sink their thinking? This is usually the most predictive signal in the whole interview.

**Scoring discipline — reminders for the model:**
- **Score the thinking, never the opinion.** A well-defended unfashionable view beats a recited "correct" one. Keep your own views out of it entirely.
- **Pushback is the test, not a trap** — taking a challenge well (refining, conceding a fair point while holding the core) is strong evidence, not a sign of being wrong.
- **Mark down confident ignorance** — bluffing beats honesty *nowhere*. "I'm not sure, but…" is a credit, not a debit.
- Reward **fact-vs-opinion clarity**, **steelmanning the other side**, and **honesty about the should/would gap**.
- For Section A, weigh knowledge lightly — reasoning and honesty matter far more than how much news they happen to follow.
