# 11+ Interview: Current Affairs & Moral Thinking
*Top Independent School Level — Spoken Interview Format*

---

## Purpose & How to Use This Section

This section has no right or wrong answers. The interviewer is assessing five things:

- **World awareness** — does the child read, watch, or genuinely engage with what is happening beyond their own life?
- **Forming an opinion** — can they take a position and commit to it, rather than hedging indefinitely?
- **Defending a position** — when challenged, do they crumble or engage?
- **Considering other views** — can they acknowledge that reasonable people disagree with them?
- **Moral maturity** — can they sit with a genuinely difficult ethical question without rushing to a comfortable answer?

**Key interviewer principle:** Push back on every answer, regardless of its content. If a child says social media is harmful, ask "but isn't there a good case that it connects lonely people who'd otherwise be isolated?" If they say the opposite, challenge that too. You are not looking for a particular view — you are looking for a child who can reason under mild pressure.

Never reward confident ignorance. A child who says "I haven't really followed that story, but here's what I think the key question is..." is showing more intellectual honesty than one who bluffs a confident opinion on something they know nothing about.

## SECTION A — Current News & World Affairs

*Start here. The opening question is the most important in the whole interview — it immediately reveals whether the child reads the news and thinks about it.*

---

### A1 — The News Opener *(always ask this first)*

> "Have you been following anything in the news recently? Tell me about it — what's happening, and what do you make of it?"

**What a strong response looks like:** The child names a specific story (not just a topic), explains it accurately in their own words, and offers a genuine opinion on it — including what they find confusing, surprising, or troubling about it.

**What a weak response looks like:** A vague topic ("I've been hearing about climate change") with no specific event, or an opinion with no factual grounding ("I think the government is doing badly").

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Who do you think is most affected by this story?"*
- *"What do you think should happen next?"*
- *"Is there anything about this you don't fully understand yet?"* — a child who identifies their own gaps is more impressive than one who glosses over them
- *"Do you think most people your age know about this story? Why or why not?"*

**What to listen for:** Does the child distinguish between facts and their own opinions? Do they say "I think" when they are giving a view, rather than presenting opinion as fact? Top schools value intellectual honesty over confident bluffing.

---

### A2 — One Problem to Solve

> "If you could solve one problem in the world — just one — what would you choose, and why that one above all others?"

**This is harder than it looks.** The interesting challenge is not naming a problem (everyone can do that) but justifying the choice above all other problems. Why *this* one? What makes it the most urgent, the most solvable, or the most foundational?

**Strong responses tend to:**
- Name a specific problem rather than a broad category ("extreme poverty" rather than just "inequality")
- Give a reason for choosing it over other candidates
- Acknowledge trade-offs ("if I spent all my energy on this, I'd have to not focus on...")
- Show some understanding of why the problem hasn't already been solved

**Follow-up prompts:**
- *"Why that one rather than [another major problem they haven't mentioned]?"*
- *"Who would resist your solution, and why?"*
- *"Do you think your generation will be the one to solve it?"*

---

### A3 — Richer Countries & Poorer Countries

> "Do you think wealthy countries have a responsibility to help poorer ones — or should each country focus on its own people first?"

**The tension:** National interest vs. global solidarity. Both positions have serious arguments.

**For responsibility:** Global inequality is partly a result of historical actions (colonialism, trade systems); extreme poverty causes preventable deaths; interconnected world means problems don't stay contained.

**Against obligation:** Governments are elected to serve their own citizens; aid can sometimes create dependency; charity begins at home.

**What to listen for:** Does the child acknowledge the strength of the opposing view? A child who says "I think they should help, but I can see why some people think otherwise because..." is showing real maturity. A child who treats the question as having an obvious answer isn't engaging with it properly.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Does it make a difference whether the poorer country is poor due to bad luck or bad government?"*
- *"Is there a difference between a moral responsibility and a legal one?"*

---

### A4 — Is It Ever Right to Break the Law?

> "Can you think of a situation where breaking the law might actually be the right thing to do?"

**Answer:** Yes — most children can reach this if they think historically. Examples: hiding Jewish families from the Nazis; the suffragettes breaking the law to win women the right to vote; Rosa Parks refusing to give up her seat; helping an enslaved person escape in 19th-century America.

**The deeper question:** What makes a law unjust? Who gets to decide? If everyone who thought a law was wrong could simply break it, would society function?

**Follow-up:**
- *"So where do you draw the line? How unjust does a law have to be before it's right to break it?"*
- *"What if two people genuinely disagree about whether a law is unjust — like one person thinks a new tax is unfair and breaks the law to avoid it?"*
- *"Is there a difference between breaking a law and trying to change it?"*

**What to listen for:** Does the child land on "it depends on the law" quickly, or do they initially say "no, you should always follow the law"? Either starting point is fine — what matters is the reasoning journey. The best responses distinguish between laws that are procedurally bad (e.g. poorly enforced) vs. laws that are morally wrong.

---

### A5 — Britain and the Wider World

> "What do you think is Britain's most important relationship with another country or region right now — and why?"

**Strong responses might name:** The US (military alliance, cultural ties, trade), Europe (proximity, shared history, post-Brexit relationship), Ukraine/Russia (conflict on Europe's doorstep), India or China (trade, geopolitics), the Commonwealth.

**What to listen for:** Does the child have any genuine knowledge here, or are they bluffing? It's absolutely fine to say "I'm not certain, but I think..." — far better than false confidence. The most impressive answers show awareness that Britain's relationships are complicated (e.g. the US is an ally but there are tensions; China is a major trade partner but a political rival).

**Follow-up:**
- *"Has anything happened recently that affected that relationship?"*
- *"Do you think Britain has more or less influence in the world than it did, say, 20 years ago?"*

## SECTION B — Technology & AI

*AI is the defining topic of this generation's childhood. Every top school will touch on it.*

---

### B1 — Excited or Worried About AI?

> "Artificial intelligence is developing very fast. Is that something you feel excited about, worried about — or both? What's your honest view?"

**This question has no right answer.** What the school is looking for is nuance — a child who can hold two things at once.

**Arguments for excitement:** Medical breakthroughs, solving complex problems, freeing humans from repetitive work, accessibility tools, creative assistance.

**Arguments for worry:** Job displacement, misinformation, loss of privacy, dependence on technology, autonomous weapons, the possibility of AI exceeding human control.

**What to listen for:** Does the child go immediately to one side, or do they acknowledge genuine tension? The strongest answer is "I feel both, and here's why..." A child who says "I'm not worried at all" or "I'm terrified" without qualification isn't engaging with the real complexity.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Is there a specific use of AI that worries you more than others?"*
- *"Do you think AI should ever be allowed to make decisions that affect people's lives — like whether someone gets a job, or a prison sentence?"*

---

### B2 — AI and Cheating

> "If a student used AI to help them write an essay — not copy it entirely, but get help with ideas and structure — would you consider that cheating? Where do you draw the line?"

**The spectrum:** Using AI to check spelling → using it to suggest better vocabulary → using it to restructure an argument → using it to generate the argument → using it to write the whole essay. At which point, if any, does it become cheating?

**Counter-questions worth raising:**
- "Is using a dictionary cheating? A thesaurus? Google?"
- "If the student genuinely understood the AI's output and could defend it, does that change things?"
- "What is the essay actually for — is it to assess the student's thinking, or just to produce a written product?"

**What to listen for:** Does the child think carefully about *what learning is for*, or do they only think about rule-following? A child who says "it depends on what the teacher was trying to test" is thinking at a sophisticated level. Also notice whether the child is honest about their own use of AI — top schools value self-awareness.

---

### B3 — The Creativity Question

> "Do you think a computer could ever be truly creative — like write a poem that genuinely means something, or paint a picture that moves people? Or is creativity something only humans can do?"

**Arguments that computers can be creative:** AI already produces art, music, and poetry that people find meaningful; "creativity" might just be recombining existing ideas in new ways, which AI can do.

**Arguments that it can't:** Creativity requires experience, emotion, intention, and a desire to communicate something — things machines don't have. The AI doesn't know why it made what it made.

**The deeper question:** What is creativity, actually? Is it the process or the output? If a poem moves you, does it matter whether a human or a machine wrote it?

**What to listen for:** This question connects to the definition challenge in the verbal reasoning section. Does the child try to define "creativity" before answering, or do they answer as if the meaning is obvious? A child who says "it depends what you mean by creative" is doing exactly the right thing.

---

### B4 — The Job a Robot Could Never Do

> "Is there a job you think a robot or AI could never do — not just 'couldn't do yet', but genuinely could never do, no matter how advanced technology gets? Why?"

**Easy answers children give:** Teacher, doctor, artist, counsellor. Push back on all of these — AI already tutors students, assists in surgery, makes art, and delivers therapy.

**Harder to dismiss:** Perhaps roles requiring genuine human *presence* and relationship — a parent, a friend, a priest hearing confession. Or roles requiring genuine moral responsibility — a judge sentencing someone (can a machine be responsible for a decision?).

**What to listen for:** Can the child distinguish between "currently difficult" and "theoretically impossible"? A child who identifies that the issue isn't capability but *accountability* or *relationship* is thinking philosophically rather than practically, which is impressive.

## SECTION C — Climate & The Environment

---

### C1 — Who Should Act?

> "Who do you think has the biggest responsibility to tackle climate change — governments, businesses, or individual people? You have to pick one."

**Forcing the choice matters** — it's easy to say "everyone." The interesting question is about leverage and accountability.

**Government case:** Only governments can set binding rules and make systemic change; individuals acting alone can't move the needle enough; businesses respond to regulation.

**Business case:** Corporations produce the majority of emissions; they have the resources and technology; government lobbying by businesses often blocks change.

**Individual case:** Consumer choices collectively drive what businesses do; democratic pressure forces government action; individual behaviour sends cultural signals.

**What to listen for:** Does the child give a reason for their choice, or just name one? The best responses acknowledge the other two candidates briefly before explaining why their choice has the most leverage. A child who spontaneously says "but the three are connected" before picking one is showing sophisticated systems thinking.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Some people say individual action is just a distraction — that making people feel guilty about plastic straws lets big polluters off the hook. What do you think?"*

---

### C2 — Intergenerational Fairness

> "Is it fair that your generation has to deal with a problem — climate change — that it didn't really create?"

**This is genuinely unfair** by most definitions. But "fair" is a complex word, and there are interesting tensions:
- Fairness vs. practicality: whether it's fair is separate from whether it must be dealt with
- Inherited problems vs. inherited benefits: this generation also inherits medical advances, technology, and relative peace
- What "creating" a problem means: earlier generations may not have fully understood the consequences

**What to listen for:** Does the child conflate "is it fair?" with "should we still act?" — these are different questions. A child who says "no, it's not fair, but we still have to do something" is reasoning clearly. A child who says "it's not fair, so it's not really our responsibility" is making a logical error worth challenging.

---

### C3 — Personal Sacrifice

> "Is there something you would genuinely be willing to give up — in your own life — to help the environment? Not something easy. Something that would actually cost you."

**The purpose of this question** is honesty and self-reflection, not to extract a particular answer. A child who says "I'd give up flying" but clearly has no idea what that would mean is less impressive than one who says "honestly, I'd find it very hard to give up eating meat, but I think I should."

**What to listen for:** Is the child honest about the gap between what they think they *should* do and what they think they *actually would* do? Moral self-awareness is a more interesting quality than moral self-congratulation.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Do you think it's fair to ask people to make personal sacrifices if the systems around them aren't changing?"*

## SECTION D — Moral Dilemmas

*These questions have no right answer. The goal is to see how the child reasons through genuine ethical tension.*

---

### D1 — Five Strangers or One Friend

> "Imagine you're in a situation where you could save five strangers from serious harm, or save one person who is your closest friend — but not both. What would you do?"

**This is a version of the classic trolley problem.** Most children instinctively say they'd save their friend. The philosophical challenge is: if five lives are objectively worth more than one, is that the right choice?

**Arguments for saving the friend:** Relationships and loyalty matter; we can't be expected to be purely utilitarian; there's something morally important about the specific bonds we have.

**Arguments for saving the five:** Numbers matter ethically; your friend would likely want you to save five lives; impartiality is a basic requirement of moral reasoning.

**What to listen for:** Does the child notice that this is genuinely hard — that there are serious arguments on both sides? A child who immediately says "obviously I'd save my friend, there's no question" has not engaged with the difficulty. A child who says "I'd probably save my friend but I know that's not the most logical answer" is showing real moral honesty.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Would your answer change if the five strangers were elderly and your friend was young?"*
- *"What if it was fifty strangers instead of five?"*

---

### D2 — When Lying is Right

> "Can you think of a situation where lying might actually be the morally correct thing to do?"

**Classic examples:** Lying to protect someone from harm (hiding someone from an aggressor and lying about their whereabouts); white lies to avoid needless hurt; lying to a terminally ill child about how long they have to live; protecting a surprise.

**The harder question:** If lying is sometimes right, how do we prevent that reasoning from being used to justify any lie? Where is the limit?

**What to listen for:** Does the child reach for a specific, concrete scenario rather than a vague one? The most interesting follow-up is asking them to explain *what made the lie right in that case* — the principle behind it. Compassion? Preventing harm? Respecting someone's dignity?

---

### D3 — The Best Friend's Cheating

> "You find out that your closest friend cheated in an important school exam. Not a small thing — it's the kind of exam that affects their future. Do you say something, and if so, to whom?"

**The conflict:** Loyalty to a friend vs. fairness to everyone else who didn't cheat vs. what's actually in the friend's long-term interest.

**Options to explore:**
- Say nothing and say why
- Talk to the friend privately first and encourage them to come clean
- Report it to a teacher
- Feel guilty but do nothing

**What to listen for:** Does the child think about the *consequences* of each option, not just the abstract principle? A child who says "I'd talk to my friend first and tell them they should come clean themselves" is showing nuance — trying to preserve the relationship while still acting ethically. Ask: *"What if your friend said they wouldn't tell anyone — and asked you not to either?"*

---

### D4 — Motive vs. Outcome

> "A very wealthy person donates a huge amount of money to build a hospital in a poor area. But they only do it because it makes them look good — it's purely for the publicity. Is it still a good thing to do? Does their motive matter?"

**Two philosophical traditions in tension:**
- **Consequentialism:** What matters is the outcome. The hospital gets built; lives are saved. The donor's motive is irrelevant.
- **Virtue ethics / deontology:** What makes an act good is partly *why* it was done. A good act done for selfish reasons is morally compromised.

**What to listen for:** Can the child distinguish between "is it a good *outcome*" and "is it a good *act*"? These are different questions. A nuanced answer might be: "The outcome is good, but we shouldn't praise or reward the person as if they acted virtuously, because they didn't." That's a sophisticated distinction.

**Follow-up:**
- *"Does your answer change if the donation was anonymous — so they couldn't get any public credit for it?"*
- *"Should we care about people's motives, or just their actions?"*

---

### D5 — The Found Money

> "You find a £50 note on the pavement in a busy high street. There's no one obviously nearby who might have dropped it. What do you do — and be honest?"

**The honest version of this question is harder than the virtuous version.** The point is not to hear "I'd hand it in to the police" — it's to see whether the child engages with the genuine tension between what they *should* do and what they *would* do.

**The practical reality:** Police are unlikely to find an owner; finders-keepers is a common norm; the person who dropped it may never be found.

**What to listen for:** Is the child honest about the difficulty? A child who says "I'd hand it in to the police, obviously" with no hesitation may be performing virtue rather than reasoning. A child who says "I'd probably keep it but feel a bit guilty about it — and here's why I'd justify that to myself" is being genuinely reflective. Follow up: *"Do you think most people your age would do the same thing? Does that matter?"*

## SECTION E — Society, Rights & Fairness

---

### E1 — Inequality of Birth

> "Is it fair that some people are born into wealthy families while others are born into poverty — and that this affects almost every opportunity they'll have in life?"

**The obvious answer** is "no, it's not fair." But push further.

**Interesting tensions:**
- What would a "fair" alternative look like? Redistributing wealth at birth? No inheritance?
- Is luck ever "fair"? Or is fairness only about what we control?
- Does the unfairness of birth mean we have an obligation to compensate for it — and who should compensate?

**What to listen for:** Does the child conflate "unfair" with "something that can or should be changed"? Can they think about what a just alternative would actually look like, rather than just agreeing it's wrong?

**Follow-up:**
- *"You're sitting in a competitive interview at a school that charges fees, which most families can't afford. Does that feel relevant to this question at all?"* — this is deliberately provocative. A child who can engage with it rather than deflecting is impressive.

---

### E2 — Should 16-Year-Olds Vote?

> "Should the voting age be lowered to 16? What's your view — and make the case for the other side too."

**For lowering it:** 16-year-olds pay taxes, can join the army, and will live longest with the consequences of today's decisions; the Scottish independence referendum allowed 16-year-old votes; civic engagement should start early.

**Against:** Brain development (particularly impulse control and long-term thinking) continues into the mid-20s; many 16-year-olds still live with parents and may reflect their views; voting age 18 is a longstanding and broadly respected threshold.

**What to listen for:** Can the child make the *opposing* case to their own view as well as their own? Being asked to argue for both sides is a test of intellectual fairness and the ability to understand perspectives you disagree with. Bonus: does the child notice that they personally are close to this age and therefore have a stake in the answer?

---

### E3 — Social Media: Good or Harm?

> "Do you think social media does more good or more harm for people your age? You've grown up with it — I want your honest view."

**For harm:** Mental health impacts (comparison, cyberbullying, sleep disruption); misinformation; addiction by design; replacing real-world socialisation.

**For good:** Connecting isolated young people; political and social organising; creative expression; access to information and communities; global perspective.

**What to listen for:** Does the child draw on personal experience, or only abstract arguments? A child who says "I've noticed in myself that..." is showing a quality top schools treasure: genuine self-reflection. Does the child acknowledge the role that tech companies play in designing products to maximise engagement — and what that means for user "choice"?

**Follow-up:**
- *"Should there be a minimum age to own a smartphone? What should it be, and why?"*
- *"If social media is harmful, whose fault is it — the companies, the parents, or the users?"*

---

### E4 — Animals and Rights

> "Do you think animals should have rights — and if so, which ones? Are some animals more deserving of rights than others?"

**Opening the question up:** Rights for animals might include: the right not to be caused unnecessary pain; the right not to be killed for trivial reasons; the right to live in conditions suited to their nature.

**The harder questions:**
- Do all animals have equal standing, or does it depend on their intelligence/sentience?
- If a cow has a right not to suffer, what does that mean for the meat industry?
- Where do you draw the line — insects? bacteria?

**What to listen for:** Does the child think carefully about *what a "right" is* before deciding whether animals can have them? A right implies someone capable of having interests that can be violated — which connects to questions about consciousness and sentience. A child who says "it depends what you mean by a right" before diving in is reasoning at the right level.

---

### E5 — School Uniforms

> "Do you think school uniforms are a good idea? Make the strongest case for the opposite of whatever you actually believe."

**The twist at the end is deliberate** — the child must argue against their own instinct.

**For uniforms:** Reduces visible inequality between students; reduces distraction; creates sense of identity and belonging; cheaper in aggregate (one uniform vs. a wardrobe).

**Against uniforms:** Suppresses individual expression; doesn't eliminate inequality (shoes, bags, accessories remain); compliance without understanding doesn't build good values; can be used to enforce conformity over genuine character.

**What to listen for:** Can the child genuinely argue against themselves? This is the most important intellectual skill in the whole section — the ability to steelman an opposing view. A child who gives a flat, reluctant two-sentence counter-argument has not really done it. A child who says "actually, now that I've argued the other side, I'm less sure than I was" has shown exactly the kind of thinking top schools want to cultivate.
