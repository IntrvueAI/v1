# 11+ Interview: Mathematical Thinking Questions
*Top Independent School Level — Spoken Interview Format*

---

## Purpose & Assessment Criteria

These questions assess four qualities, **not** just correct answers:

- **Numerical Reasoning** — can the child work accurately and flexibly with numbers?
- **Estimation** — can they reason about magnitude and make sensible approximations?
- **Structured Thinking** — do they break problems into steps, organise their approach?
- **Explanation Quality** — can they articulate *why* an answer is true, not just state it?

Interviewers should prompt freely: *"Tell me what you're thinking." / "How did you get there?" / "Is there another way to see it?"* A child who reasons aloud and reaches a wrong answer often deserves more credit than one who gives the right answer with no explanation.

---

## SECTION A — Numerical Reasoning

*Questions that test flexible, careful work with numbers — fractions, digit properties, remainders, and operations.*

---

### A1 — Digit Reversal

> "I am thinking of a two-digit number. If you reverse its digits, you get a number that is 27 more than my original number. Also, the two digits add up to 11. What is my number?"

**Answer:** 47

**Working:** The tens digit and units digit add to 11. Reversing the number increases it by 27. If the digits are *a* and *b*, then 10b + a − (10a + b) = 27, so 9b − 9a = 27, meaning b − a = 3. Combined with a + b = 11: b = 7, a = 4. The number is 47 (reversed: 74, difference: 27 ✓).

**What to listen for:** A child using algebra is impressive but not required. More interesting is whether they reason: "the digits differ by 3 and add to 11 — so they must be 4 and 7." Trial and improvement is fine, but look for *systematic* checking rather than random guessing.

---

### A2 — Three-Digit Mystery

> "I am a three-digit number. My hundreds digit is three times my units digit. My tens digit is one more than my units digit. The sum of all three of my digits is 16. What number am I?"

**Answer:** 943

**Working:** Let the units digit = u. Then hundreds = 3u, tens = u + 1. Sum: 3u + (u + 1) + u = 5u + 1 = 16, so 5u = 15, u = 3. Units = 3, tens = 4, hundreds = 9. The number is **943**.

**What to listen for:** Does the child name a variable (or call it "something") and build equations from the clues? Or do they work by trial? Either is valid — the key is whether they set up all three constraints before testing values.

---

### A3 — Sharing Remainders

> "Four friends are dividing a pile of sweets equally, but there are 2 left over. A fifth friend then arrives, and when they share equally between five people, there are 3 left over. What is the smallest number of sweets the pile could contain?"

**Answer:** 18

**Working:** Find the smallest number that leaves remainder 2 when divided by 4, and remainder 3 when divided by 5.

Numbers leaving remainder 2 ÷ 4: 2, 6, 10, 14, **18**, 22…  
Numbers leaving remainder 3 ÷ 5: 3, 8, 13, **18**, 23…  
First number in both lists: 18.

**What to listen for:** A child who writes out both lists and looks for overlap is thinking well. Watch whether they understand *what a remainder is* — some children confuse it with the quotient. Asking "can you check your answer?" is useful here.

---

### A4 — The Persistent Adder

> "Starting from 1, I keep adding the next odd number each time. So I have: 1, then 1 + 3 = 4, then 4 + 5 = 9, then 9 + 7 = 16, and so on. After I have added the 20th odd number, what is my total?"

**Answer:** 400

**Why:** The sum of the first *n* odd numbers always equals n². The sequence 1, 4, 9, 16… are perfect squares. After 20 odd numbers: 20² = 400. (The 20th odd number is 2 × 20 − 1 = 39.)

**What to listen for:** Does the child spot the pattern (1, 4, 9, 16 are perfect squares) from the examples given in the question? Can they generalise? A strong candidate notices the pattern immediately; a good candidate spots it after writing out a few more terms. Credit anyone who sees it, even if they can't explain why it works.

---

### A5 — The Sale Price Trap

> "A coat normally costs £80. In a sale, the price is reduced by 20%. After the sale ends, the shop puts the price back up by 25%. What is the new price? Is it more than, less than, or the same as the original £80?"

**Answer:** Exactly £80 — the same as the original.

**Working:** 20% off: £80 × 0.8 = £64. Then 25% on: £64 × 1.25 = £80.

**Why these two percentages cancel:** 25% of £64 = £16. £64 + £16 = £80. The reason 20% down and 25% up cancel is that 1/5 down brings you to 4/5, and then 1/4 up from there brings you back to the whole. (4/5 × 5/4 = 1.)

**What to listen for:** Almost every child says "they should cancel but I'm not sure which way." Very many say the final price is more (because 25 > 20). The insight is that the 25% is applied to a *smaller* number. A child who says "you can't just add percentages because they're of different amounts" is thinking at an excellent level.

---

### A6 — Chickens and Rabbits

> "A farmer counts all the animals in his yard. He has only chickens and rabbits. He counts 20 heads and 56 legs. How many chickens and how many rabbits does he have?"

**Answer:** 12 chickens, 8 rabbits.

**Working (elegant method):** Imagine all 20 animals are chickens: that gives 40 legs. But we have 56 legs, so 16 legs are "unexplained." Each rabbit has 2 more legs than a chicken, so the number of rabbits = 16 ÷ 2 = 8. Chickens = 20 − 8 = 12.

**Check:** 8 × 4 + 12 × 2 = 32 + 24 = 56 ✓

**What to listen for:** The "all chickens" method is elegant and shows a child who creates a baseline and adjusts. Trial and improvement works but is slower. Some children set up two simultaneous equations (c + r = 20; 2c + 4r = 56) — this is impressive. Ask *"can you explain why your method works?"* regardless of approach.

---

### A7 — The Fuel Tank

> "My car's fuel tank is three-quarters full. During a long drive, I use two-thirds of the fuel that was in the tank at the start of the journey. What fraction of a full tank do I have left?"

**Answer:** 1/4

**Working:** Start with 3/4 of a tank. Use 2/3 of that: 2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2. Remaining: 3/4 − 1/2 = 3/4 − 2/4 = **1/4**.

**What to listen for:** The key distinction is "two-thirds of what is *in the tank*" versus "two-thirds of the full tank." A child who computes 2/3 of the full tank (giving 2/3 used, leaving 1/4... wait, that gives 1/3 − something, which doesn't parse) has misread the question. Ask them to re-read it slowly. A child who multiplies fractions correctly and then subtracts is handling this well.

## SECTION B — Estimation & Order of Magnitude

*Questions that test whether a child can reason sensibly about size and scale, making and justifying approximations.*

---

### B1 — The Average Speed Trap

> "I drive from London to Edinburgh, a journey of 400 miles. On the way there I average 50 miles per hour. On the way back the roads are busier and I only average 40 miles per hour. What is my average speed for the whole round trip? Have a think before you answer."

**Answer:** 44.4 mph — not 45 mph.

**Working:** Total distance = 800 miles. Time there = 400 ÷ 50 = 8 hours. Time back = 400 ÷ 40 = 10 hours. Total time = 18 hours. Average speed = 800 ÷ 18 ≈ 44.4 mph.

**Why 45 mph is wrong:** You spend more *time* driving at 40 mph than at 50 mph, so the slower speed has a bigger pull on the average. Averaging the speeds directly only works when equal *times* are spent at each speed — here it's equal *distances*.

**What to listen for:** Nearly everyone says 45 mph. The child who pauses and thinks "but I spend longer driving slowly, so shouldn't it be below 45?" has excellent instincts. The formula insight — total distance ÷ total time — is the key.

---

### B2 — Heart Beats in a Year

> "Roughly how many times does your heart beat in a year? I don't want an exact answer — just a sensible estimate. Talk me through how you'd work it out."

**A reasonable path:**
- Heart rate ≈ 70 beats per minute (accept 60–80)
- Minutes per day: 60 × 24 = 1,440
- Beats per day ≈ 70 × 1,440 ≈ 100,000
- Days per year ≈ 365
- Total ≈ 100,000 × 365 ≈ **37 million beats per year**

**What to listen for:** This is entirely about process. Does the child break it into stages? Do they choose sensible approximations and say why? Do they sanity-check ("that seems plausible")? A child who says "I don't know how fast hearts beat" can be told — what matters is whether they then use that information structurally.

---

### B3 — Stack of a Million Pounds

> "If you stacked one million £1 coins in a single pile, roughly how tall would it be? Would it be about as tall as a house, a skyscraper, or a mountain?"

**A reasonable path:**
- A £1 coin is about 3 mm thick
- 1,000,000 × 3 mm = 3,000,000 mm = 3,000 m = **3 km**
- The Burj Khalifa (tallest building) is ~830 m — our stack is much taller
- Mount Snowdon is ~1,085 m — still shorter than our stack
- Ben Nevis is ~1,345 m — still shorter
- Mont Blanc is ~4,800 m — taller than our stack
- So: taller than any skyscraper, taller than British mountains, but shorter than the Alps

**Answer: roughly 3 km — between a skyscraper and a large mountain.**

**What to listen for:** Does the child know a £1 coin is a few millimetres thick? (If not, say "assume it's about 3 mm.") The key skill is unit conversion: mm → m → km. A child who gets confused going from mm to km is showing a gap in numerical fluency worth noting.

---

### B4 — Counting to a Million

> "If you counted out loud at one number per second — one, two, three... — how long would it take to count from 1 to 1,000,000? Give your answer in a sensible unit."

**Answer:** About 11.5 days of non-stop counting.

**Working:** 1,000,000 seconds ÷ 60 = 16,667 minutes ÷ 60 = 278 hours ÷ 24 ≈ **11.6 days**.

**What to listen for:** Most children guess "a few hours" or "a few days." The interest is whether they correctly convert seconds into a meaningful unit. Does the child realise there are 86,400 seconds in a day? (60 × 60 × 24 — they may need prompting to derive this.) A child who works through the unit conversion systematically is demonstrating excellent structured thinking.

---

### B5 — The Birthday Puzzle

> "In a class of 30 children, what do you think is the probability that at least two of them share the same birthday? Would you say it is more likely than not, or less likely than not? Why do you think so?"

**Answer:** More likely than not — the probability is approximately 70%.

**Why:** This is the famous Birthday Problem and is deeply counterintuitive. With 30 people, the probability that all birthdays are different is roughly 29% — so the chance of at least one shared birthday is about 71%. The reason is that with 30 people there are 30 × 29 ÷ 2 = 435 different *pairs* of people who could share a birthday. That's a lot of chances.

**What to listen for:** This question is not about calculation — it's about probabilistic intuition and argumentation. A child who says "there are loads of pairs, so it's probably more likely than you'd think" is reasoning beautifully. A child who says "there are 365 days and only 30 people, so it's unlikely" — a very common and understandable mistake — should be asked: "how many *pairs* of people are there?"

## SECTION C — Structured Problem Solving

*Multi-step problems where the path to the answer requires systematic organisation.*

---

### C1 — The Locker Problem

> "There are 20 lockers in a row, all starting closed. The first student walks past and opens every locker. The second student walks past and closes every second locker — locker 2, locker 4, locker 6, and so on. The third student changes the state of every third locker. This continues until the twentieth student has walked past. At the end, how many lockers are left open?"

**Answer:** 4 lockers — numbers 1, 4, 9, and 16.

**Why:** A locker ends up open if it is toggled an odd number of times. A locker is toggled once for each of its factors. Only perfect squares have an odd number of factors (because one factor pairs with itself — e.g. 9 has factors 1, 3, 9, and 3 × 3 = 9 means 3 is its own pair). So only lockers in perfect-square positions stay open.

**What to listen for:** A child who says "let me try locker 1, then locker 2, then locker 3 and see what happens" is taking exactly the right approach. The leap to "perfect squares" may not come immediately — what matters is whether they can find the pattern from cases.

---

### C2 — The Painted Cube

> "I make a large cube out of 27 smaller cubes — it is three cubes wide, three cubes tall, and three cubes deep. I dip the whole thing in red paint, so the entire outside is covered. Then I break it apart into all 27 small cubes. How many of the small cubes have paint on exactly two faces?"

**Answer:** 12

**Why:** Imagine the positions: 8 corner cubes have 3 painted faces. 12 edge cubes (along each of the 12 edges of the large cube, each edge contributes 1 non-corner cube) have exactly 2 painted faces. 6 face-centre cubes have 1 painted face. 1 centre cube has 0 painted faces. Total: 8 + 12 + 6 + 1 = 27 ✓.

**What to listen for:** Does the child visualise the cube systematically by thinking about *where* each small cube sits? Using hands to point to corners, edges, and faces is a very good sign. A child who jumps to 12 without checking the other cases should be asked to verify the total adds to 27.

---

### C3 — The Running Track

> "A standard running track has two straight sections and two semicircular ends, making a complete loop. Each straight section is 80 metres long. The total length of one lap around the outside is 400 metres. What is the distance from one straight edge to the other — the width of the track?"

**Answer:** Approximately 76.4 m (exactly 240/π metres).

**Working:** The two straights contribute 2 × 80 = 160 m. The remaining 400 − 160 = 240 m comes from the two semicircular ends, which together form one complete circle. So the circumference of that circle is 240 m. Since C = πd, the diameter = 240/π ≈ 76.4 m.

**What to listen for:** The key decomposition step — realising the two semicircles together make one circle — is the crux. Does the child set up the problem by subtracting the straight sections first? Do they know the circumference formula? If they don't know π ≈ 3.14, that's fine — they can leave the answer as 240/π and explain what they've done.

---

### C4 — The Handshake Problem

> "At a party, every person shakes hands with every other person exactly once. There are 45 handshakes in total. How many people are at the party?"

**Answer:** 10 people.

**Working:** With n people, the number of handshakes = n(n − 1) ÷ 2. Setting this equal to 45: n(n − 1) = 90. Testing n = 10: 10 × 9 = 90 ✓.

**Alternatively:** The first person shakes 9 hands, the second shakes 8 new hands (already shook with person 1), the third shakes 7 new hands, and so on: 9 + 8 + 7 + 6 + 5 + 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 45 ✓.

**What to listen for:** Does the child recognise that each handshake involves two people, so you can't just multiply? Do they approach it systematically — either by formula or by the "each new person shakes with all previous" method? Both are excellent.

---

### C5 — The Village Well

> "A village has one well. Every day, four families each draw the same amount of water from it. The well refills itself at a constant rate overnight. If six families lived in the village instead of four, the well would run dry after exactly 12 days. If only two families lived there, the well would never run dry — in fact it would slowly overflow. Assuming the well starts full, how many days would it last with four families?"

**Answer:** 36 days.

**Working:** Let C = capacity of the well, r = daily refill rate, d = daily usage per family.

With 6 families: daily net loss = 6d − r. Well runs dry in 12 days: 12(6d − r) = C → 72d − 12r = C.

The "2 families never run dry" condition means 2d ≤ r. The borderline case — just barely never running dry — is r = 2d.

Substituting r = 2d into the first equation: 72d − 12(2d) = C → 72d − 24d = C → C = 48d.

With 4 families: daily net loss = 4d − 2d = 2d. Days until empty = 48d ÷ 2d = **36 days**.

**What to listen for:** This is one of the harder questions. Award credit for any structured approach — naming variables, correctly interpreting "never runs dry," or getting partway through. A child who correctly identifies that 2 families is the break-even point is showing excellent insight.

---

### C6 — Tournament Matches

> "A knockout tennis tournament has 64 players. Every match has one winner and one loser, and the loser is immediately eliminated. How many matches need to be played in total before there is one champion?"

**Answer:** 63 matches.

**Why (elegant):** Each match eliminates exactly one player. To go from 64 players to 1 champion, exactly 63 players must be eliminated. Therefore exactly 63 matches must be played — regardless of the bracket structure.

**What to listen for:** The brute-force approach (64 → 32 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1, counting 32 + 16 + 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 = 63) gives the right answer and is fine. But the elegant argument — "each match eliminates exactly one person, so you need 63 matches to eliminate 63 people" — shows a child who reasons from *invariants*. Ask "can you think of a quicker way to see it?" to prompt this.

---

### C7 — The Broken Calculator

> "My calculator is broken. The only working buttons are: 1, +, −, and =. I can press them as many times as I like. What is the fewest number of button presses needed to display the number 13?"

**Answer:** 7 presses: `1`, `1`, `+`, `1`, `1`, `+`, `1` `=`... actually let's be precise.

The key is to use `11` as a building block. 11 + 1 + 1 = 13. Button presses: `[1]` `[1]` `[+]` `[1]` `[+]` `[1]` `[=]` = 7 presses.

Can we do fewer? With 6 presses (5 entries + `=`): the maximum reachable would be something like `1 1 + 1 1 [=]` = 22, but we need 13. `1 + 1 1 + 1 [=]` = 14. `1 1 + 1 - 1 [=]` = wait that needs a `-`, giving 11 + 1 − 1 = 11, not 13. 6 presses cannot reach 13.

**Answer: 7 presses.**

**What to listen for:** Does the child realise they can form the number 11 (by pressing 1 twice)? This "building two-digit numbers" insight is the crux. Do they consider subtraction as a route? A child who systematically eliminates 6-press options before settling on 7 is showing rigorous thinking.

---

### C8 — The Camel and the Desert

> "A man has 3,000 bananas and a camel. He wants to transport as many bananas as possible across a 1,000-mile desert. The camel can carry at most 1,000 bananas at a time, and eats exactly one banana per mile it walks. What is the maximum number of whole bananas the man can arrive with at the other side?"

**Answer:** 833 bananas (technically 833⅓, but since bananas are whole, 833).

**Key insight — using depots:** The camel must make multiple trips for the first part of the journey. For the first section (miles 0 to 200), the camel makes 3 forward trips and 2 return trips = 5 legs of 200 miles = 1,000 bananas eaten, leaving 2,000 at mile 200. From mile 200 to 533⅓, make 2 forward trips and 1 return = 3 legs. From mile 533⅓ to 1,000, carry the remaining 1,000 bananas in one trip.

**What to listen for:** Don't expect a full solution. Watch whether the child understands *why* the camel must make multiple trips. Do they figure out that leaving stockpiles at intermediate points helps? Do they see that the camel eating bananas on return trips is a real cost? Award credit generously for structural insight, even without a final answer.

## SECTION D — Pattern, Proof & Explanation Quality

*Questions that test whether a child can spot a rule, articulate it clearly, and begin to explain why it must be true.*

---

### D1 — The Lily Pad

> "On the first of June, there is a single lily pad on a pond. Every day, the number of lily pads exactly doubles. By the thirtieth of June, the pond is completely full. On which date was the pond exactly half full?"

**Answer:** The 29th of June.

**Why:** Since the lily pads double every day, the day before the pond was full, it was exactly half full. This works backwards from the answer — no calculation needed.

**What to listen for:** Many children try to work *forwards*: "on day 1 there's 1, day 2 there's 2, day 3 there's 4…" and want to calculate 2²⁸ or 2²⁹. The beautiful insight is to work backwards: *"Whatever the pond looks like on the 30th, on the 29th it must have been half of that."* A child who says this immediately has excellent mathematical instincts. Ask anyone who gives the right answer: "can you explain that without any calculation?"

---

### D2 — The Staircase

> "A staircase has 10 steps. I can climb it by taking either one step or two steps at a time, in any combination I like. For example, I could take 10 single steps, or 5 double steps, or some mixture. How many different ways are there to climb the staircase?"

**Answer:** 89 ways.

**Why (Fibonacci):** Let f(n) = the number of ways to climb n steps. On the first move, you either take 1 step (leaving f(n−1) ways to finish) or 2 steps (leaving f(n−2) ways). So f(n) = f(n−1) + f(n−2). With f(1) = 1 and f(2) = 2:

f(3)=3, f(4)=5, f(5)=8, f(6)=13, f(7)=21, f(8)=34, f(9)=55, f(10)=**89**.

**What to listen for:** Expect most children to start listing combinations. The insight is to notice that the number of ways to reach step *n* depends only on steps *n−1* and *n−2* — once seen, this builds the sequence quickly. A child who discovers this structure and says "each answer is the sum of the two before" is showing exceptional mathematical maturity. Don't expect this — reward it highly if it appears.

---

### D3 — Consecutive Numbers

> "Is it always true that if you add two consecutive whole numbers together, the answer is always odd? Can you convince me it must always be true — not just with examples, but with a reason?"

**Answer:** Yes, always odd.

**Why:** Any two consecutive whole numbers contain exactly one even number and one odd number. Even + odd = odd. Always.

**Algebraic version:** If the first number is n, the next is n + 1. Their sum is 2n + 1, which is always odd (it's one more than an even number).

**What to listen for:** Most children will test a few examples (2+3=5, 4+5=9) and say "yes it seems true." The real challenge is the *explanation*. Can the child say "one of them must be even and one must be odd, and those always add to an odd"? That is the key logical step. A child who uses n and n+1 to write 2n+1 is thinking algebraically — remarkable at 11+.

---

### D4 — Reverse and Subtract

> "Take any two-digit number — let's say 73. Reverse the digits to get 37. Now subtract the smaller from the larger: 73 − 37 = 36. Try this with a few other two-digit numbers. What do you notice? Can you explain why?"

**Examples:** 82 − 28 = 54. 91 − 19 = 72. 51 − 15 = 36. 63 − 36 = 27.

**Pattern:** The answer is always a multiple of 9. More specifically, it equals 9 times the difference of the two digits.

**Why:** A two-digit number with tens digit *a* and units digit *b* equals 10a + b. Its reverse is 10b + a. The difference: (10a + b) − (10b + a) = 9a − 9b = 9(a − b). Always a multiple of 9.

**What to listen for:** This question has three stages — noticing the pattern, stating it precisely, and explaining it. A child who reaches stage 1 ("they're all multiples of 9") is doing well. Stage 2 ("it's 9 times the difference of the digits") is excellent. Stage 3 (the algebraic explanation) is exceptional. Ask "why do you think that happens?" after each stage to push them further.

---

### D5 — Square Numbers from Odd Numbers

> "We noticed earlier that 1 = 1, 1 + 3 = 4, 1 + 3 + 5 = 9, 1 + 3 + 5 + 7 = 16. These are all perfect squares. Can you think of a picture or a reason why adding the first *n* odd numbers always gives you *n* squared?"

**The visual argument:** Draw a 1×1 square. Adding a border of 3 tiles around it gives a 2×2 square. Adding a border of 5 tiles gives a 3×3 square. Each border is L-shaped and contains the next odd number of tiles. So the n×n square is built by layering n such L-shapes: 1 + 3 + 5 + … + (2n−1) = n².

**What to listen for:** This is an open-ended question. Some children will want to draw. Encourage it. The L-shape insight is beautiful and accessible — a child who discovers it is making a genuine mathematical observation. Even a child who says "each time you're adding a border, and the border gets bigger each time" is close to the right idea.

---

### D6 — Folded Paper

> "I take a square piece of paper and fold it in half three times. I then make one straight cut all the way through. When I unfold the paper, what is the maximum number of holes I could have made?"

**Answer:** 8 holes.

**Why:** Each fold doubles the number of layers. After 3 folds: 2³ = 8 layers. A single straight cut through all 8 layers, positioned so it doesn't land on a fold line, creates 8 holes when unfolded.

**What to listen for:** Does the child connect "3 folds" to "8 layers" via powers of 2? Do they reason about *where* the cut must land to create the maximum holes (not on a fold)? A child who asks to use a piece of paper to experiment is showing good scientific instinct — allow it.
